Classification, the foundational stage of genocide, involves the societal construction of distinct groups, fostering an "us versus them" mentality. This division is often based on inherent or perceived differences such as ethnicity, race, religion, or nationality. While the act of categorizing people is a natural human tendency, it becomes dangerous when these classifications are rigidified, imbued with negative stereotypes, and used to establish hierarchies of power and worth. This initial separation lays the groundwork for dehumanization and the justification of violence against the "other" group.

The process of classification is often fueled by historical narratives, cultural biases, and political rhetoric that emphasize the differences between groups while downplaying shared humanity. Dominant groups may actively promote these divisions to maintain their power or to scapegoat minority groups for societal problems. Language plays a crucial role in this stage, as specific terms and labels are used to define and often denigrate the targeted group, creating a sense of inherent difference and otherness in the minds of the broader population.

Without this initial act of classification, the subsequent stages of genocide become significantly more challenging to implement. By establishing clear boundaries between groups and assigning distinct characteristics, often negative ones, the perpetrators begin the process of psychologically separating the targeted population from the rest of society. This mental separation makes it easier to deny their humanity and ultimately to justify their persecution and extermination.

The Nuremberg Laws enacted in Nazi Germany serve as a chillingly precise example of the classification stage. These laws, passed in 1935, did not simply identify Jews; they legally and systematically defined Jewishness based on ancestry, effectively creating a distinct and legally inferior racial category. This went beyond religious affiliation, encompassing individuals with Jewish grandparents even if they did not practice Judaism. The legal framework established a clear "us" (Aryan Germans) and "them" (Jews), codifying a racial hierarchy that placed Jews at the bottom.

Furthermore, the Nuremberg Laws included specific prohibitions designed to enforce this separation. The "Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honor" forbade marriages and extramarital relations between Jews and Germans, aiming to prevent the perceived "contamination" of the Aryan race. This legislation reinforced the notion that Jews were a fundamentally different and undesirable group, biologically distinct from the "pure" German population. The legal entrenchment of these classifications provided the state with the authority to systematically discriminate against and isolate Jewish citizens.

The impact of the Nuremberg Laws extended far beyond legal definitions. They permeated social and cultural life, reinforcing existing antisemitic prejudices and creating an environment where discrimination was not only legal but also socially acceptable, and even encouraged. By legally defining and isolating Jews as a separate and inferior race, the Nazi regime laid the essential groundwork for the subsequent stages of persecution, stripping Jews of their rights and dignity and paving the way for their eventual systematic annihilation.
